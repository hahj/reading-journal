Notes on Resilience

Where is there resilience in the novel? Resilience can be seen in many ways. It can be viewed as large, dramatic uprising. But often, for the people involved, it lies quietly within their own mental space. The most obvious examples are Offred's memory (of her past), Moira's several attempts at escaping from the Red Center and the underground May Day network.

How do the characters demonstrate resilience? The two best examples of Offred demonstrating Resilience are simply telling stories herself. When she records her thoughts while hiding from Gilead, she is showing them that they will never take away who she was. Moira shows Resilience physically and verbally by openly rebelling against the Aunts, in an attempt to escape the Red Center, which could result in torture.

Link to themes: Resilience has a very strong relationship with the idea of Language as Power. In a place where reading and writing are forbidden to women, Offred's inner thoughts and desires to play Scrabble are forms of survival.

Author’s message: Atwood also seems to indicate that Resilience is not always about the outcome. Often times, just keeping your mind from being surrendered to those oppressing you is enough to win.

Significant Quotations:

  • • "I intend to last" (Atwood 8).
  • • "Nolite te bastardes carborundorum" [Don't let the bastards grind you down] (Atwood 52).
  • • "I tell, therefore you are" (Atwood 268).
